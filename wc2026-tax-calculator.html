❓ Frequently asked questions
Everything you need to know about US sports betting taxes
Do I have to pay tax on WC 2026 betting winnings? +
Yes. The IRS treats gambling winnings as ordinary taxable income. This includes winnings from sportsbooks like DraftKings, FanDuel, and Caesars. You must report winnings even if the sportsbook doesn't send you a W-2G form. All gambling winnings go on Schedule 1, Line 8b of your Form 1040.
Can I deduct my losses? +
Yes — but only if you itemize deductions (Schedule A). You can deduct gambling losses up to the amount of your gambling winnings. You cannot deduct losses that exceed your winnings. Keep records of all bets placed (date, type, amount, location). Most people take the standard deduction, in which case losses can't be deducted.
What is a W-2G form? +
A W-2G is issued by the sportsbook when you win $600 or more AND the payout is at least 300x your wager. DraftKings, FanDuel, and other major books send these automatically by January 31. You still must report all winnings — the IRS receives copies of W-2Gs and will cross-reference your return.
Do states tax gambling winnings differently? +
Yes, significantly. Nine states have no income tax at all (Texas, Florida, Nevada, etc.) — no state tax on winnings. Most states use a flat rate. New York has the highest effective rate (up to 10.9% state + NYC local). California taxes all income at up to 13.3% but does NOT allow deduction of gambling losses at the state level.
What if I win big from a parlay? +
Parlay winnings are fully taxable as ordinary income. A $10 parlay that wins $10,000 means $10,000 in taxable income. If the payout is 300x or more your wager AND exceeds $600, the sportsbook will issue a W-2G. The full winnings amount is taxable — not just the profit.
Should I consult a tax professional? +
This calculator provides estimates for informational purposes only. For significant winnings (generally $5,000+), consulting a CPA or tax professional is strongly recommended. State laws vary, and individual circumstances (other income, filing status, deductions) significantly affect your actual liability.
